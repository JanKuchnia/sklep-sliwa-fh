## Anti-Patterns Verdict

**LLM assessment**: This reads less like "AI made this" than a typical generated landing page, mainly because the content is real and specific (actual SKUs, DIN/EN specs, netto/brutto math) rather than generic marketing copy. That said, three structural tells are present: the boxed, uppercase, tracked hero eyebrow ("TRADYCJA I JAKOŚĆ W RZESZOTARACH") sitting above the H1; a 4-up row of identical icon-circle + heading + text feature cards; and directly below it, a 5-up row of near-identical icon-circle + heading + caption category cards. Individually minor, but stacked on one screen they're exactly the "eyebrow + identical card grid" reflex the project's own DESIGN.md Do's and Don'ts explicitly bans.

**Deterministic scan**: `detect.mjs` flagged `flat-type-hierarchy` on `index.html` line 282 — most font sizes in use cluster between 13.1px and 16px (14 distinct sizes), with only one outlier at 22.4px, for a total range ratio of 1.7:1. Sidebar filter labels, footer links, and body copy sit close enough in size that their semantic differences don't read visually. No false positives; this matches what's visible in the screenshots (filter list, footer columns, and card body copy all sit at a similar visual weight).

**Browser evidence** (agent-browser, static site served locally; Chromium via CDP): Confirmed the eyebrow/card-grid pattern visually. More importantly, it surfaced two real functional bugs the static read-through missed:
- Of 18 `<img>` elements sampled across the homepage and catalog, 9 failed to load (`naturalWidth: 0`) because every product photo is hotlinked to Unsplash with no local fallback — a user on a flaky connection or behind a filtering proxy sees blank/broken boxes where product photos should be, with no placeholder styling to soften it.
- The one image that *did* load for `p-201` ("Łańcuch Odbierakowy Ogniwowy Ocynkowany DIN 766 6mm" — a zinc-plated steel chain) rendered a photo of steamed dumplings/dim sum, completely unrelated to the product. This is a genuine content-accuracy defect, not a network flake: the Unsplash photo ID assigned to that SKU simply depicts the wrong subject.
- On a mobile viewport (iPhone 14 emulation), the category sub-nav scrolls horizontally with the "BHP & Odzież Robocza" category clipped off-screen and no visual cue (fade, arrow) that scrolling reveals more items.

## Overall Impression

A functionally coherent prototype with an honest, trade-specific voice — the netto/brutto toggle and real SKU-level specs do real work toward the "sturdy, no-nonsense, trustworthy" brand goal. That trust is undercut by the product imagery: nearly half the sampled images don't load at all, and the one product where a chain photo actually rendered showed food instead. For a business whose whole pitch is "we have real stock, come see it," a customer hitting a wrong or broken photo on the very product they searched for is the single most damaging thing on the site today. The generic eyebrow-badge-plus-card-grid opening is a secondary, lower-stakes issue.

## What's Working

1. **The B2C/B2B price-mode switch** is a genuinely distinctive interaction, not a bolted-on toggle — orange stays exclusive to retail actions, blue stays exclusive to wholesale ones, so a user can tell which pricing lane they're in from color alone, matching the "Two-Lane Rule" the design system commits to.
2. **Copy discipline**: no marketing buzzwords anywhere in the catalog. Product descriptions cite real specs (norms, dimensions, materials) instead of vague adjectives, which is exactly right for a hardware wholesaler's credibility.
3. **Empty states are handled**, not ignored: the zero-search-results state and the empty-cart state both pair an icon with a specific message and a next action, which most prototypes at this stage skip entirely.

## Priority Issues

**[P0] Product images unreliable, and at least one is factually wrong**
Why it matters: For a wholesaler whose core pitch is "the stock is real, come see it," a broken or mismatched product photo directly undercuts the one thing the site needs to prove. Half the sampled images (9/18) failed to load with no fallback styling; the one image that loaded for the steel-chain SKU showed a food photo.
Fix: Source real (or at minimum topically correct, reliably-licensed) product photography and host it locally instead of hotlinking Unsplash IDs at runtime. Add a CSS fallback (tinted background + a generic tool/chain/paint icon matching the category) for any `<img>` that fails to load, via `onerror` or a `<picture>`/background-image pattern, so a network hiccup never shows a blank box.
Suggested command: `/impeccable harden` (production-readiness pass on the image-loading path), followed by `/impeccable polish` once real assets exist.

**[P1] Hero eyebrow + duplicate icon-card rows read as templated**
Why it matters: PRODUCT.md and DESIGN.md both explicitly commit to avoiding "generic SaaS/AI-slop" — this is exactly that reflex, stacked twice on the homepage (feature cards, then category cards, both icon-circle-over-text).
Fix: Drop the boxed uppercase hero tag or fold its message into the H1/subhead copy directly. Give the feature-card and category-card rows visibly different structures (e.g., one as a horizontal stat/fact list, the other kept as the clickable category grid) so the page doesn't repeat the same card shape twice in a row.
Suggested command: `/impeccable quieter` (the eyebrow badge), `/impeccable layout` (differentiate the two card rows).

**[P1] Flat type hierarchy confirmed by the detector**
Why it matters: Font sizes cluster at 13–16px across body copy, filter labels, and footer links; only the hero heading breaks the pattern. Readers can't distinguish primary from secondary text by size alone, working against the "clarity over decoration" principle in PRODUCT.md.
Fix: Widen the scale — push secondary/label text down toward ~0.7rem and give section subheads (currently 0.95rem `.filter-group-title`) more separation from body text (1rem), per DESIGN.md's own ≥1.25 step-ratio rule.
Suggested command: `/impeccable typeset`.

**[P2] `javascript:` URI links for category shortcuts**
Why it matters: Every category quick-link (nav bar, footer, home category cards use `onclick` but nav/footer use `href="javascript:filterCategory(...)"`) breaks "open in new tab," fails silently with JS disabled, and is a known anti-pattern that static analysis tools flag.
Fix: Replace with `<button>` elements calling `filterCategory()`, or real `#catalog` hash links carrying a query param the router reads.
Suggested command: `/impeccable audit`.

**[P2] No mobile affordance for the overflowing sub-nav**
Why it matters: On a phone, the category row scrolls horizontally with the last category (BHP & Odzież Robocza) clipped off-screen and zero visual hint that more items exist — a distracted one-handed shopper (per the Casey persona) may never discover it.
Fix: Add an edge fade-mask or a small chevron affordance when `scrollWidth > clientWidth`.
Suggested command: `/impeccable adapt`.

## Persona Red Flags

**Jordan (First-Timer)**: The B2C/B2B toggle assumes prior knowledge of "netto/brutto" wholesale-pricing convention. A first-time retail visitor sees "Hurt (Netto)" with no inline explanation of what qualifies someone for wholesale pricing or whether it's open to anyone who clicks it — the toggle works, but its meaning isn't taught anywhere on the page.

**Casey (Distracted Mobile User)**: Product "Dodaj" buttons sit within thumb reach at the bottom of each card — good. But the horizontally-scrolling category sub-nav has no visible affordance that it scrolls, confirmed in the mobile screenshot where "Ogrodnicze" clips at the viewport edge; Casey, scanning one-handed, would likely never scroll far enough to find Narzędzia Ręczne or BHP.

**Riley (Stress Tester)**: Directly caught two real defects: roughly half of all product images fail to load with zero fallback UI, and the one image that did render for the steel-chain product showed steamed dumplings — a feature that "appears to work" (an `<img>` tag renders something) but produces a visibly wrong result, exactly the kind of silent-failure Riley is built to catch.

## Minor Observations

- Cart-drawer submit button is clickable with an empty cart; only a post-click `alert()` catches it, rather than the button being disabled proactively.
- The cart drawer's inline qty stepper has no ceiling tied to `stockQty`, unlike the single-product subpage's `max="${product.stockQty}"` input — an inconsistency between the two "add quantity" paths.
- Order/quote confirmations use native `alert()` dialogs, which feel jarring against the otherwise designed UI; a toast or inline confirmation would match the system better.
- Detector-flagged font clustering also affects filter-sidebar labels and footer link columns, which read at nearly the same visual weight as body copy despite different semantic roles.
