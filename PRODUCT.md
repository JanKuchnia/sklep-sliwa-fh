# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Two overlapping audiences: local tradesmen and contractors around Rzeszotary / Świątniki Górne / Skawina who buy in bulk (chains, fittings, fasteners) and want fast price/stock checks before driving to the warehouse for pickup; and individual retail customers browsing garden, construction, and hand tools who compare prices and want to know what's in stock before visiting. Both are walk-in/pickup shoppers, not remote-checkout buyers — the site's job is to get them to call or drive over with confidence, not to complete a transaction online.

## Product Purpose

A static prototype storefront for Śliwa FH (Firma Handlowa Czesław Śliwa), a garden/construction hardware wholesaler in Rzeszotary, Poland. It presents the product catalog with dual B2C (brutto/retail) and B2B (netto/wholesale) pricing, lets people browse by category, search, and build a cart that becomes a pickup reservation or a wholesale quote request — no real payment or delivery. Success means a visitor trusts the business is well-stocked and legitimate, quickly finds what they need, and either calls or shows up.

## Brand Personality

Sturdy, no-nonsense, trustworthy. This is a working wholesaler, not a lifestyle brand — the design should read as competent and durable rather than polished-for-polish's-sake. Confidence comes from clarity (real stock counts, real prices, an easy-to-find phone number and address) rather than from visual flourish.

## Anti-references

Avoid the generic SaaS/AI-slop landing-page look: no gradient text, no tiny uppercase tracked eyebrows above every section, no numbered 01/02/03 section scaffolding, no identical icon+heading+text card grids, no cream/sand/parchment default palette. This is a hardware wholesaler, not a startup — the design should not read as a templated pitch deck.

## Design Principles

1. Clarity over decoration — stock counts, prices (netto/brutto), and contact info are the actual value; never let visual style bury them.
2. Trade-honest tone — plain Polish trade terminology, no marketing buzzwords, no invented specs or reviews (per INFO/research.md, several facts like hours and exact address are still unverified and must not be dressed up as certain).
3. Dual-audience by design — B2C and B2B are both first-class, not one bolted onto the other; price mode switching should feel deliberate, not like an afterthought toggle.
4. Built for the drive-over, not the checkout — every flow should end in "call" or "pick up in Rzeszotary," since there's no real payment/delivery behind this prototype.
5. Sturdy over trendy — industrial-practical materials (solid color, clear type, real borders) over glassmorphism, soft gradients, or novelty motion.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Standard WCAG AA. Given a trade customer base that may skew older, favor larger touch targets, strong body-text contrast, and interactions that don't depend solely on hover.
